SEMINAR DESCRIPTION/GOALS:

What is the meaning of life? Is there life after death? What effect will my choices and actions here and now have on my future existence? Can and will I be saved? How will that happen? Questions like these have been considered by philosophers and theologians throughout history. How exactly do they arrive at their answers? What kinds of methods and evidence do they use to investigate, support and explain their answers? What do the most influential accounts look like?

This seminar will examine four of the most influential answers to the preceding questions by exploring the teachings of the Socrates, Buddha, Confucius, and Jesus. It will also consider how Eastern and Western views of salvation are distinct and related. As a result, this seminar is designed to provide a structured opportunity for philosophical and theological reflection on four of the most influential accounts of the meaning and purpose of life and what happens afterward. It will help students develop: 1. a vocabulary of terms and concepts for considering "salvation" questions, 2. a framework for critically evaluating the strengths and weaknesses of four of the most influential answers to these questions, and 3. a personal understanding of the effects of these views on both individual human actions and society.

 

COURSE STRUCTURE:

The seminar will be divided into four parts. We will begin by considering various philosophical and theological notions of "the meaning and purpose of life" and "salvation." The first part will then be concerned with the life, beliefs, and arguments of Socrates. The second part will be concerned with the teachings of the Buddha and Buddhist conceptions of "nirvana." The third part will focus on Confucius and other Chinese conceptions of the goal of life. The fourth part will be concerned with the teachings of Jesus.

Students will be assigned weekly readings, and be asked to participate in an on-line discussion of the readings. Class time will be devoted to discussion and to student presentations based on the readings.

A final paper will be required of each student. A topic will be selected early in the class and students will accumulate material from each section of the course to incorporate in their final paper.

ASSIGNMENTS:

Reading of assigned materials is a MUST in order to introduce you to the subject matter, facilitate class discussion and participation, pose distressing questions, and just plain make you think. The course will begin with a lecture format (roughly 2 weeks) as we present an overview of the lives and thought of Socrates, Buddha, Confucius, and Jesus. It will then proceed in seminar style with SIGNIFICANT emphasis on student participation. Students will be responsible for four areas of activity: 1) Class Participation, 2) Web Participation, 3) Discussion Leading, and 4) a Final Paper.

1) PARTICIPATION includes class discussion of the assigned readings and Web postings at the Blackboard Threaded Discussion Area arranged for the seminar. Students MUST post at least one reaction to the readings each week.

2) DISCUSSION LEADING consists of choosing a date and selected reading and meeting with Dr. Landry & Dr. Laumakis to discuss the readings (at least one week before your topic is to be discussed), posting questions and comments for discussion on the Web prior to your discussion, and leading class discussion.

3) THE FINAL PAPER consists of an extended (10? page) discussion and evaluation of some topic or theme treated in the seminar. Late papers will be penalized one full grade for each day they are late.
