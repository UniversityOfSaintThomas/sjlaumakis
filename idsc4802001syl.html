SEMINAR DESCRIPTION/GOALS:

Twenty-five years ago in "Sports in America" James Michener wrote, "Sports have become a major force in American life. We devote more money and time to them than we realize. They consume a major portion of our television programming, and our newspapers allocate tremendous space to their coverage.Their effect upon our schools and colleges is oftentimes deplorable, and they have been accused of generating at least some of the violence that assaults us." Recent events at the University of Minnesota and other incidents involving both amateur and professional athletes seem to confirm Michener's claims.

Sports are obviously a significant feature of our society. In fact, almost all Americans are at some time or another participants in or spectators of sports. However, we very rarely pause to reflect upon the meaning and significance of these activities-for ourselves or for society. As a result, this seminar is designed to provide a structured opportunity for philosophical reflection on the nature, history, and practice of sports in America. It will help students develop: 1. a vocabulary of terms and concepts for the philosophical study of sports, 2. a framework for critically evaluating the strengths and weaknesses of the practice of sports, and 3. a personal understanding of the effects of sports on society.

 

SEMINAR STRUCTURE:

The seminar will be divided into roughly four parts. The first part will be concerned with specifying the basic principles, definitions and distinctions among such key terms as "play," "sport," and "games." It will also consider the various ways of participating in sports and play. The second part will be concerned with the relationship between sports and society. In addition to who plays and why, this part will consider the effects of sports on social institutions. The third and largest part of the seminar will be concerned with ethical issues in sports. It will consider the role and participation of women in sports, sports and the media, money and financing, and the role of government in organized sports. The impact of sports on colleges and universities will receive special attention. The fourth part will be concerned with the effect of sports on individuals. It will consider questions about how sports leads to self-knowledge, the mind and body relationship, sports and aesthetics, why individuals participate in sports and the role of sports throughout life.

Students will be assigned weekly readings, and be asked to participate in an on-line discussion of the readings as well as prepare a one-page reaction paper for class each week. Class time will be devoted to discussion and to student presentations based on the readings.

We will also have several guest speakers including: the UST athletic director, coaches from men's and women's teams, and a member of the local media.

ASSIGNMENTS:

Reading of assigned materials is a MUST in order to introduce you to the subject matter, facilitate class discussion and participation, pose distressing questions, and just plain make you think. The course will begin with a lecture format (roughly 4 weeks) as we present an overview of Philosophy of Sports. It will then proceed in seminar style with SIGNIFICANT emphasis on student participation. Students will be responsible for four areas of activity: 1) Participation, 2) Weekly Reaction Papers, 3) Discussion Leading, and 4) a Final Project/Paper.

1) PARTICIPATION includes class discussion of the assigned readingsand Web postings at the Threaded Discussion Area arranged for the seminar. The site is at: webcampus5.stthomas.edu/webct/public/show_courses.pl?964095575

Students MUST post at least one reaction to the readings each week.

2) WEEKLY REACTION PAPERS are 1 TYPED PAGE in length and are based on the pre-assigned readings or some aspect of the Threaded Discussion Area postings for the week. Students responsible for Discussion Leading in class will be exempt from the Weekly Reaction Papers during that week. They will, however, be responsible for significant Web postings.

3) DISCUSSION LEADING consists of forming a group of 3 students (by WEEK #3), selecting a topic (by WEEK #3), meeting with Mr. Riverso & Dr. Laumakis to discuss the readings (at least two weeks before your topic is to be discussed), posting questions and comments for discussion on the Web prior to your discussion, and leading class discussion during the week you present the issues involved in your topic.

4) THE FINAL PROJECT/PAPER consists of either a synthesis of your Weekly Reaction Papers into a coherent account of some topic or theme treated in them or an extended (5? page) discussion and evaluation of some topic or theme treated in the seminar. Late papers will be penalized one full grade for each day they are late.
